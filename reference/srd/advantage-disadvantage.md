# Advantage and Disadvantage

Source: *System Reference Document 5.2* ("SRD 5.2"), provided by Wizards
of the Coast LLC under the Creative Commons Attribution 4.0 International
License (CC-BY-4.0). This file quotes/paraphrases the relevant rules text
for DM reference during play; see `reference/srd/README.md` for the full
attribution and scope note.

Sometimes a D20 Test (ability check, saving throw, or attack roll) is
modified by Advantage or Disadvantage. Advantage reflects positive
circumstances around the roll; Disadvantage reflects negative
circumstances. A creature usually acquires either through a special
ability, action, or condition (see `conditions.md`) — or the GM can decide
circumstances grant/impose one directly.

## Rolling two d20s

When a roll has either Advantage or Disadvantage, roll a second d20 along
with the first:

- **Advantage** → use the **higher** of the two rolls.
- **Disadvantage** → use the **lower** of the two rolls.

Example: with Disadvantage, rolling an 18 and a 3 → use the 3. With
Advantage on the same two rolls → use the 18.

## They don't stack

- If multiple situations grant Advantage on the same roll, still roll only
  two d20s total (not three or more).
- Same for multiple sources of Disadvantage — still only two d20s.
- **If circumstances cause a roll to have both Advantage and
  Disadvantage, the roll has neither — roll only one d20.** This holds
  even if several things impose Disadvantage and only one thing grants
  Advantage (or vice versa): any presence of both cancels to neither.

## Interactions with rerolls

If a creature has Advantage or Disadvantage and something (e.g. Heroic
Inspiration) lets it reroll or replace the d20, only **one** of the two
dice may be rerolled/replaced, not both — the player/creature chooses
which one.

## Common conditions that impose Disadvantage or Advantage

See `conditions.md` for the full definitions; the ones most likely to come
up mechanically during play:

- **Blinded**: attack rolls against the blinded creature have Advantage;
  its own attack rolls have Disadvantage.
- **Frightened**: Disadvantage on ability checks and attack rolls while
  the source of fear is in line of sight.
- **Invisible**: attack rolls against the invisible creature have
  Disadvantage; its own attack rolls have Advantage (unless the target can
  somehow see it).
- **Poisoned**: Disadvantage on attack rolls and ability checks.
- **Prone**: Disadvantage on its own attack rolls; attack rolls against it
  have Advantage if the attacker is within 5 feet, Disadvantage otherwise.
- **Restrained**: Disadvantage on its own attack rolls and Dexterity
  saving throws; attack rolls against it have Advantage.
- **Paralyzed / Petrified / Stunned / Unconscious**: attack rolls against
  the creature have Advantage (Unconscious/Paralyzed also grant automatic
  Critical Hit if the attacker is within 5 feet).
